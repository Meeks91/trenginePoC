Salha Aziz had long wanted to be a professional fitness creator on TikTok and Instagram, but was missing one big thing: a large audience.
"I was just there for fun," said Aziz, who has under 5,000 followers on her TikTok account @sociallyaziz. "I wished that I could make money doing something I loved, which is being on social media and making videos."
Then, earlier this year, she found out about UGC, short for user-generated content, and realized she didn't need a substantial audience to make money from her content — she just needed to be good at selling.
Brands have been using user-generated product reviews for promotional purposes for decades. But [several ad agencies told Insider](https://www.businessinsider.com/tiktok-ugc-boom-for-creators-earn-money-cheap-for-advertisers-2022-7) that they have noticed brands' increased interest in UGC to be used directly on the their social-media accounts or in paid ads.
Part of the recent surge in demand can be credited to TikTok, where UGC often takes the form of people casually filming themselves, recommending a product or showing how to use it. Many brands see this kind of content as feeling authentic and native to the platform.

This authenticity aspect drew Aziz to UGC.
"You're scrolling, and it just looks like another person on your feed," she said. "It doesn't look like an ad."
Like Aziz, some creators see UGC as a way to monetize their content creation skills even when they don't have a significant following on social media.
Those who already have bigger followings [turn to UGC to reduce the amount of sponsored content](https://www.businessinsider.com/how-much-personal-finance-tiktok-creator-earns-spends-a-month-2022-7) on their personal accounts.
For brands, UGC is significantly cheaper than partnering with creators for sponsored content — an attractive incentive in the current uncertain economy.
A report [published in March](https://www.businessinsider.com/how-much-influencers-are-paid-per-sponsored-post-tiktok-youtube-2022-3) found that influencers with less than 10,000 followers charged an average of $901 per sponsored post in 2021, and that rate increased to $3,000 for creators with an audience ranging from 50,000 to 200,000 followers.
UGC, meanwhile, can cost between $125 to $500 per post, or sometimes nothing at all, Jessica Romaniuk, Chicago president and director of media, social, and PR at ad agency Two by Four, [previously told Insider](https://www.businessinsider.com/tiktok-ugc-boom-for-creators-earn-money-cheap-for-advertisers-2022-7).
Aziz began pitching brands for collaboration in June, four months after discovering what UGC was and doing her research about the industry. 
**This is what she charges for UGC, as of August:**
  * $160 for one video
  * $450 for three videos
  * $750 for five videos 
  * A $850 bundle package with five videos and 10 photos


The rates can change, she said, depending on how long a brand wants to partner for.
**And here's how much Aziz booked in UGC contracts in the past three months:**
  * June: $830
  * July: $6,295
  * August: $4,095


Insider verified the information with documentation Aziz provided. Aziz clarified that her August earnings are for contracts she signed, but has not yet received payment for. 
Some of her brand partners include haircare brand Goldie Locks, bone broth company Broya, and showerhead manufacturer Jolie.
In May, Aziz also started her own TikTok account documenting her progress in building a business as a UGC creator.
In her videos, she shows both successes and struggles. 
She says she spent about four months researching UGC before trying it herself. And it took month for her to start making money.
"You have to have structure, a business structure and a plan," she said. "It may look like it's easy because it's a 30-second video, but there's so much work that goes into it that I don't think people are realizing."
