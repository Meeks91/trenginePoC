## 2/ Attitude vs Altitude
Altiture Centre
Advertisement - Continue Reading Below
Living and training in thin air has worked wonders for generations of athletes from mountainous areas across the globe. But after two decades on the fringes, hypoxic training, which simulates the effect of exercising in high-altitude environments, is starting to gain steam among mountaineers and normies alike.
Originally developed to help climbers prepare for the rigours of time spent on the highest of peaks, hypoxic therapy is now being retooled as part of a wider biohacking breakthrough. [Studies](https://www.medsci.org/v20p1551.htm?utm_source=chatgpt.com) show that time spent in controlled low-oxygen environments can bring about huge physiological benefits both up in the sky and at sea level.
Unlike restrictive oxygen masks, altitude training can more accurately simulate the exact conditions found on the world’s highest mountains. Training can involve anything from exercising or spending time in a specially designed chamber to simply sleeping in an altitude tent (think: a [sauna](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/health/a60547378/sauna-benefits/), but at 8,000m).
‘The appeal here is twofold,’ says _MH_ fitness director Andrew Tracey. ‘We’ve seen the effects of doing [slower sessions](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/fitness/a64604529/slow-running-training/) and [zone-2 cardio](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/fitness/cardio-exercise/a61134886/zone-2-training/) in an altitude chamber on things such as red and white blood cell count, so lots of gyms are rushing to get these technologies available to their members.
‘At the same time, it’s also making sports such as mountaineering far more accessible. Hypoxic training is helping to democratise the ability to go and do these things if you don’t have a huge budget or the time,’ says Tracey. ‘You can now almost bypass the expensive process of hiking and acclimatising to these environments.’
As evidence, Tracey points to the boys from outerwear brand ThruDark who, with hypoxic training, [completed a trip from London to the top of Everest in just seven days](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/fitness/a65168977/everest-training-plan-special-forces-london-summit/).
**TRY IT:** The [Altitude Centre](https://altitudecentre.com) provides a range of cutting-edge hypoxic equipment throughout the UK, including altitude tents, portable chambers, training plans and in-house sessions.
## 3/ From the Track to Trail
The rise of the run club in recent years has been celebrated and spoofed almost to the point that they now sit beyond parody. But while their popularity shows no signs of waning, runners bored of social [5ks](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/fitness/cardio-exercise/a69053608/average-5k-time-for-men-training-tips/) will embrace new ways to pound the ground in 2026.
Clubs celebrating different styles of running are on the rise. Social track club Track Mafia has built a devoted following on the pursuit of speed, running weekly sessions at west London’s Paddington Recreation Ground athletics track, where regular joes can arrive and, for an hour or so, turn on the afterburners with structured training sessions aimed at making everyone just that little bit more athletic.
Runners looking for new challenges are also moving away from the pavement towards green spaces for inspiration. Trail-running clubs are booming, with crews such as Wild Trail Runners hosting monthly runs beyond the city limits and through the capital’s larger parks, while road-based running clubs now routinely offer monthly trail runs to shake things up.

## 4/ A Thoughtful New Way to Eat
In a new world where diets are becoming increasingly tied to identity politics, it suddenly feels as if there are few things in wellness more polarising than the way we choose to eat. And while social media will continue to amplify extreme opinions on both sides, what if 2026 ushered in a mindful approach and brought us back together around the dinner table?
For Tracey, the new era will be defined by the philosophy of ‘contemplative nutrition’ – a term coined by Huel co-founder James Collier in his book In the book, Collier calls for a shift away from [calories](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/weight-loss/a69725757/do-you-need-to-count-calories/) and [macros](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/nutrition/a36092326/macro-calculator/), and towards a more holistic approach grounded in five key pillars: physical health, mental wellbeing, sustainability, ethics and reclaiming the act of eating as a social activity.
‘People are thinking more than ever beyond just calories in, calories out, or even the pure health implications of what they’re eating,’ says Tracey. ‘They’re thinking wider, about the ethical, environmental and ecological implications of their food.’
The stats back this up. While social media remains flooded with extreme diets, the popularity of more considered approaches, such as flexitarianism and adherence to the Mediterranean diet, has broadly grown. According to The Food Foundation, two-thirds of the UK are open to cutting down their consumption of animal products, while an Innova report found that 54% are working to reduce their consumption of processed foods.
‘This has always been seen as a privileged stance to take,’ says Tracey. ‘But, it could simply mean making small tweaks to approach our diets in a more considered way.’
## 5/ The Race to Find the Next Hyrox

Not since the rise (and fall?) of [CrossFit](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/fitness/a26387550/crossfit-complete-guide/) have we seen a group fitness trend take over gyms in the way [Hyrox](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/fitness/a69165520/average-hyrox-station-times/) has. In less than a decade, competitive fitness racing has grown into a $140m (around £105.7m) sport with one all-powerful organisation at its centre, despite a number of low-budget contenders. ‘I don’t think we’ll see Hyrox’s momentum fizzle out for at least a couple of years,’ Tracey tells us. ‘Hyrox classes and simulations are still oversubscribed to the max.’
What comes next after the inevitable boom-bust cycle? No one really seems to know, but plenty will be spending 2026 on the quest to find the industry’s next truly viral activity.
‘The race is sort of the trend itself,’ says Tracey. ‘The problem is that no one seems to be doing a particularly good job. There are other races, but they just don’t have the production value Hyrox does. As such, we’re seeing a growing number of gyms throwing their hands up and saying, “We’re just doing our own version of Hyrox because demand is so high.” So, I think while people go looking for the next big thing, in the interim we’re going to see more and more of that – people cooking up classes and experiences that complement Hyrox or offer a similar experience to those who can’t get tickets, rather than trying to replace it.’

## 9/ Clangin’ and Bangin’
We’re living an era of peak saturation when it comes to training trends. Hybrid athletes and barefoot shoes are still everywhere to be seen amid a certain age bracket. Gen Z, however, will take inspiration from a more old-school strain of gym culture to define their 2026.
‘If you look at the way 16-year-olds are training right now, they’re not hitting CrossFit gyms or functional classes,’ says Tracey. ‘TikTok gym culture is centred around [bodybuilding](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/building-muscle/a69529990/old-school-dup-method/), while young men are discovering the gym as a safe space in which to explore and express their masculinity through fitness.’
Part of this, Tracey says, comes down to bodybuilding’s cost-effectiveness compared with more expensive classes. ‘Young people just want to go into the gym, and they’re finding empowerment in that. To me, that’s genuine gym culture, and I think it’s only going to grow.’

## 10/ The Surge of Single-Session ‘Micro-Therapy’
With Gen Z raised on bite-sized TikToks and deterred by therapy costs and waiting lists, ‘short, sharp interventions look to be as routine in 2026 as meditation apps were a decade ago’, believes co-founder of Kove clinic Dr Jenna Vyas-Lee.
A [2025 umbrella analysis](https://osf.io/hcvp2/overview?utm_source=chatgpt.com) of single-session therapy (SST) found 83% of review studies reported significant positive effects in at least one area, from anxiety and depression to problematic drinking. ‘SST isn’t for complex, long-standing issues, but it can be a game changer when the issue is clear and time-sensitive,’ she details. Or when budgets won’t stretch to months of weekly sessions. That could be deciding whether to stick or twist in your career, or learning one tool to break an intrusive thought cycle (without bulldozing your budget with an extended course). Alongside digital platforms and private clinics, more NHS Talking Therapies teams are piloting solution-focused approaches.

## 12/ Mitochondria Will Have a Moment
Imagine your biology teacher’s delight to know that [mitochondria](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/health/a69643350/how-to-strengthen-your-mitochondria-energy-health/) are trending in 2026. ‘Mitochondrial health refers to how efficiently cells turn food into usable energy and manage oxidative stress,’ says Mohammed Enayat, GP and founder of Hum2n. Research shows mitochondrial function declines with age and disease, sparking demand for inclusion in biological-age tests. Dr Enayat insists lifestyle is ‘primary’ for cellular health: physical activity, sleep, diet, stress management. Clinically studied supplements include omega-3s, B vitamins and coenzyme Q10. As for buzzy NAD+ precursors, mitochondrial peptides and targeted antioxidants? ‘Many are nascent and should be used under medical advice,’ he says, putting hopes of a mitochondrial anti-ageing elixir on ice until… 2027?

## 14/ Good Vibrations
Emerge
The idea of sound healing likely won’t be new to you if you’ve spent any time in a yoga studio, but advancements in tech mean that devices harnessing the healing powers of sound and vibration are poised to become a major recovery tool.
While detailed research into the practice is still in its infancy, evidence is starting to illustrate the healing benefits of spending time exposed to low-frequency sound vibrations, with vibroacoustic devices such as sound beds and chairs linked to improvements in pain management, sleep quality, stress and anxiety.
A [trial](https://europepmc.org/article/PMC/4325896?utm_source=chatgpt.com) conducted at the University of Toronto found vibroacoustic therapy effective in treating symptoms of fibromyalgia, while others have demonstrated the grounding effects the therapy has on the autonomous nervous system, which regulates your pulse and blood pressure, and contributes to feelings such as stress and anxiety.
As such, vibroacoustic beds are now becoming an increasingly common sight in high-end gyms and recovery centres around the UK. They’re also a key pillar in the $160k (around £121k) Ammortal Chamber: a recovery super-device that resides in the training centres of the Denver Broncos, Major League Baseball’s Arizona Diamondbacks and the US Olympic squad, to name just a few.
**TRY IT:** Vibroacoustic beds are now operating at [Third Space Recovery Spa Canary Wharf](https://canarywharf.com/see-do/third-space-recovery-spa/).
## 15/ Is 2026 the Year We Finally Cure Baldness?
For well over a decade, follicularly challenged men have been resigned to one of two solutions to restore their locks; a strict regimen of sprays and pills that might not even work, or an expensive trip to Turkey. However, things might finally be set to change.

A wave of new treatments has hit clinics recently, with growing availability meaning 2026 could be the year we finally end a reliance on hair transplants. AMT therapy is one breakthrough: stem cells are harvested from the scalp and re-injected into neighbouring areas where hair loss has taken place, generating new growth. ‘As we move into 2026, diagnostics are set to become more sophisticated, improving outcomes and redefining how hair loss is treated,’ says Dr Sokratous.
Meanwhile, a new drug developed by UCLA is using genetics to bring dormant follicles back to life. Called PP405, it’s still going through testing, but a £12m investment from Google Ventures will hopefully see it gain clearance sooner rather than later.
**TRY IT:** AMT is now widely available at hair and aesthetic clinics across the UK, while services such as Hims can get you started on more conventional regrowth therapies with just a Zoom call.

## 18/ Rise (and Rise) of the Reformer
Nicola Katie
Plenty of us have been dragged to a class at some stage by our better halves, but could 2026 be the year men finally take to the [reformer](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/fitness/lifestyle/a36590832/pilates/) studio with gusto? We think so.
Reformer gyms are becoming an increasingly common sight in the gyms of some of the world’s most famous sports teams and athletes, with Mo Salah and LeBron James both noted fans. It’s not just elite athletes finding benefits from the workout, however – just ask PT Matt Kendrick, whose work getting [cover star Ed Sheeran](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/fitness/a69729547/ed-sheeran-mens-health/) into shape involved lengthy sessions on the reformer.
