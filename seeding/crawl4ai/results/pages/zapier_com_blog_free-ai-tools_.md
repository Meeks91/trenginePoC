## Best free AI search engine
### [**_Perplexity_**](http://perplexity.ai/)
**Perplexity pros:**
  * Ability to organize your searches
  * Great user experience


**Perplexity cons:**
  * Lots of duplicate features can be confusing
  * Generates some controversy


[_Perplexity_](https://zapier.com/blog/perplexity-ai/) is an AI-first search engine that rethinks what search looks like. Instead of returning a page full of blue links, it gives you a direct, conversational answer—and then lets you keep asking questions without starting over.
When you run a search, Perplexity responds with a generated summary and a follow-up box right underneath it. Because it keeps track of context, you don't need to restate your original query. If you start by asking about the iPhone's camera specs, you can follow up with something like "how's the battery life?" and Perplexity knows exactly what you mean. 
Perplexity has also gotten better at handling live events and breaking news. With the right prompt (sometimes as simple as asking what's happening "right now"), it can fetch real-time updates instead of relying solely on static sources.
To help you keep track of your research, Perplexity saves searches as threads in the sidebar. You can organize them into Spaces or turn them into Pages you can revisit or share later. There's some overlap between features with different names, but the overall effect is that your research doesn't disappear the moment you close a tab.
It's worth noting that Perplexity hasn't been without controversy. Like many AI tools, [_it's been criticized_](https://www.forbes.com/sites/rashishrivastava/2024/06/26/search-startup-perplexity-increasingly-cites-ai-generated-sources/) for occasional inaccuracies and sourcing issues, so you shouldn't treat it as a single source of truth. Our testers found it to be generally reliable—but as with any AI-powered search tool, it's best used as a starting point, not the final word.
Even as competitors catch up, Perplexity still feels like the most complete AI search experience available today. And because it [_integrates with Zapier_](https://zapier.com/apps/perplexity/integrations), you can turn research into action by automatically routing answers into docs, spreadsheets, CRMs, or Slack channels. You can learn more about [_how to automate Perplexity_](https://zapier.com/blog/automate-perplexity/), or get started with a pre-made template.
**What you get on Perplexity's free plan:** Quick searches and limited access to advanced search tools and models.

## Best free AI browser
### [**_Perplexity Comet_**](https://www.perplexity.ai/comet)
**Perplexity Comet pros:**
  * AI-powered browsing tools
  * Reliable autonomous agent


**Perplexity Comet cons:**
  * Not the most powerful AI model
  * Slightly intimidating search engine interface


[_Perplexity_](https://zapier.com/blog/perplexity-ai/)'s Comet was one of the first AI browsers to pop up, and it shows. It's easily the most polished option, and it avoids giving off the too-common impression that someone just bolted an AI feature onto an existing tool.
Comet's AI is always working in the background, not just when you ask a question. It learns how you browse and adapts in subtle ways (which, if you can get past the creepiness, is pretty useful). Tabs you're unlikely to revisit get deprioritized. Pages you tend to open at the same time each day load before you even click.
The Comet Assistant feature is a mix of a chatbot and an agent that can actually take action in your browser. Say you want to ask an Airbnb host whether the room has an office chair. Instead of opening the site yourself, you can ask Comet to handle it. It will navigate to Airbnb, open the message thread, draft a clear (and polite) note, and send it all on its own.
Comet will attempt almost anything you'd normally do in a browser, with sensible guardrails around security and payments. In that Airbnb example, it will stop at the login screen and ask you to authenticate instead of touching your password manager. I also like that you can watch everything it does in real time and pause or step in if something doesn't feel right. It's perfect for small but annoying tasks you usually avoid, like grouping dozens of open tabs or tracking down a random link from weeks ago.
On the privacy front, Comet keeps your browsing data local and only sends information to Perplexity's servers when you actively make a query. Perplexity also states it doesn't sell your data. Outside of the AI features, it feels like a normal browser: Chrome extensions work, tab groups behave as expected, and multitasking is smooth. For a free AI browser, Comet sets a surprisingly high bar.
**What you get on Comet's free plan:** Core AI browsing features, form-filling, and summaries

## Best free AI app for creating images
**ChatGPT pros:**
  * Very easy to use and a best-in-class model
  * Integrates with Zapier


**Chat cons:**
  * Very slow
  * Controls can be hit and miss


OpenAI kicked off the modern text-to-image wave with [_DALL·E_](https://zapier.com/blog/dall-e-3/), then let other image models steal the spotlight. Now it's back in a big way. The [_multimodal_](https://zapier.com/blog/multimodal-ai/) GPT-4o model can [_generate images natively_](https://zapier.com/blog/chatgpt-image-generation/) and is one of the strongest image generators you can use today.
Using it is about as simple as it gets. Just describe what you want to see, and ChatGPT generates the image directly in the chat. The free plan gets you limited image generation, which is usually enough if you're creating visuals occasionally rather than in bulk.
ChatGPT's image quality is excellent across the board. It handles text in images well, understands spatial relationships and numbers, and makes it easy to refine results with follow-up prompts. Instead of regenerating everything from scratch, you can ask it to tweak one specific detail—change a color, move an object, adjust the background—and it generally does exactly that.
ChatGPT tends to stick closely to what you asked for, whether you're aiming for something painterly, illustrative, or more photorealistic. Upload a reference image and ask for a specific style or transformation, and it does a remarkably good job. It's a noticeable improvement over DALL·E 3, which you can still access inside ChatGPT if you want to compare.
There are a few trade-offs, though. ChatGPT generates images more slowly than diffusion-based models, and it creates just one image at a time instead of multiple variations. If you're producing images at scale, that can feel limiting. But for occasional marketing graphics, concept art, or social visuals, it's rarely a dealbreaker.
Because [_ChatGPT also integrates with Zapier_](https://zapier.com/apps/chatgpt/integrations), you can automate image creation across your workflows—for example, generating images from Google Form submissions, HubSpot leads, or spreadsheet rows. You can learn more about [_how to automate ChatGPT_](https://zapier.com/blog/automate-chatgpt/) or get started with a pre-made template.
**What you get on ChatGPT's free plan:** Limited image generation with GPT-4o, the ability to edit images via follow-up prompts, and support for uploading reference images.

## Best free AI email assistant
**Shortwave pros:**
  * Deep integration with Google Calendar
  * AI assistant is very flexible


**Shortwave cons:**
  * Free plan only includes 3 months of email history


If you're looking for an AI email assistant that actually feels useful day to day, Shortwave is hard to beat. It has the broadest and most practical set of AI features I've seen in an email app, and—importantly—they're easy to access right where you already work.
Shortwave's AI lives inside a dedicated assistant you can open from the corner of your inbox. From there, you can chat with your email in plain language. And this is where Shortwave really shines. Instead of digging through old threads or guessing the right keyword, you can just ask your inbox questions like, "What did we decide on for the website redesign?" or "Find the last conversation where pricing came up." It reliably surfaces the right emails without sending you down a search rabbit hole.
That same assistant can also pull structured information out of threads (like listing tasks from a project discussion), generate new content based on your inbox ("turn my productivity emails into a tweet"), or answer questions in a ChatGPT-style back-and-forth—except the answers are grounded in your actual email history.
When it's time to reply, AI help is right where you'd expect it. From the compose area, you can generate a response from scratch or refine one based on a quick prompt, then drop it directly into your draft and keep the conversation moving.
The main limitations to be aware of: Shortwave only supports Gmail right now, and on the free plan, it only gives you three months of email history. The upgrade is pretty expensive, but if you have your email archive somewhere else, you can stick to the free plan.
