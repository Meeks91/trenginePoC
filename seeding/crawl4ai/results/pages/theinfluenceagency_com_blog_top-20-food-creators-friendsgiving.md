“Friendsgiving” first appeared online around 2007, but young people celebrated it long before then. With college, work, and life often keeping loved ones apart, friends became our Thanksgiving table.
Now, [one in five Americans](https://www.statista.com/chart/28817/thanksgiving-plans-gcs/?srsltid=AfmBOoozrngeiZ9Yag-BmJYMn69ovHhGRTJiZumHUIsgOSnJBoq1cYGB) hosts or attends a Friendsgiving celebration yearly. While the concept is laid-back, intimate, and low-pressure, you can still bring something special. ### What can I do with my Thanksgiving leftovers?
  * **Reheat and enjoy again.** As long as you properly store and freeze leftovers, you can keep eating your feast for a few days (in the fridge) to several months (in the freezer). Be sure to reheat items thoroughly to the proper temperature.
  * **Reinvent.** Make sandwiches, casseroles, soups, and pies from leftover dishes. For instant, customized inspiration, you can ask AI tools like ChatGPT or Gemini to _"create a recipe with [your leftover ingredients]”_. **Note:** AI-generated recipes may require adjustment for portion sizes and cooking times.
  * **Give away.** Send guests home with portions of leftovers in takeout containers. Unopened, shelf-stable items can also be shared, but discard anything left out too long or improperly stored. 
  * **Compost.** Scraps like vegetable peels, bones, and unused trimmings can be composted to reduce waste and create nutrient-rich soil for your garden.

### How to have a great Thanksgiving
  1. **Stop overcomplicating your menu.** Simple, comforting dishes always win.
  2. **Use what you have.** You don’t need fancy tools to make good food.
  3. **Make it cozy.** Warm lighting and good company beat perfect plating.
  4. **Don’t be scared (or ashamed) of shortcuts.** Store-bought dishes are totally fine if time, energy, or budget is tight.
  5. **Prep early.** Do the heavy lifting the day before.
