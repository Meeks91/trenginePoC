I'm generally an active person. I [strength train](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/building-muscle/) at the gym three days a week, along with weekly classes. But when [cardio](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/fitness/) gets mentioned, catch me hiding behind the dumbbell rack.
I do like [walking](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/fitness/cardio-exercise/a70011147/20-minute-walking-habit-after-40/) – but I haven't prioritised it as part of a dedicated routine. Embarrassingly, most of my walking is on my commute to work. For the next eight hours, I’m chained to the desk, other than the occasional 10-second walk to the coffee machine. During a typical week, I’ll accumulate between 1,000 to 3,500 steps Monday to Friday. I convinced myself that since I spend longer at the gym and occasionally give in to 10 minutes of hell on the StairMaster, that it's enough cardio. (It's probably not, if I'm being honest.)
Walking more has always been at the back of my mind. I did a 10K walking challenge last year, and it gave me the reality check that I generally sit down for a ridiculous amount of time. After the 30-day challenge, I kept moving, but my step count hasn't stayed consistent. So when my editor asked me to take on another viral walking routine – the "6-6-6 Challenge" that made waves on my TikTok For You Page – I was ready to give it a go. 
[walking trends](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/fitness/cardio-exercise/a65019360/5-2-4-fartlek-walking-workout/) on social media are meant to help people like me move more. Unlike the others that promised weight loss or muscle gain, however, this one centred consistency. 
## What is the 6-6-6 Walking Challenge?
The 6-6-6 walking challenge involves three components within its daily regimen. First is six minutes of warm-up stretches followed by 60 minutes of walking, and six minutes of stretching again. Ideally, these walks are slotted into your schedule at either 6 a.m. or 6 p.m., just to keep with the theme. 
At first glance, these numbers seem like a catchy way to name the trend. But [_Nick LeClair, PT, DPT_](https://www.linkedin.com/in/nick-leclair-pt-dpt-ocs-1496b1235), clinical director at ATI Physical Therapy, explains to me, each has a purpose. “The warm-up helps your body ease into movement, the steady walk builds endurance, and the cooldown helps you recover so you’re more likely to feel good afterward and stick with it over time.”

“What makes it appealing is that it gives people a simple, predictable routine,” [_Michael Wong, PhD_](https://www.unlv.edu/people/michael-wong), a kinesiology and nutrition sciences professor at the University of Nevada, Las Vegas tells _MH_. “A lot of individuals respond better to time-based prescriptions rather than general recommendations about step counts or intensity.” While the 10,000 step goal [is largely overrated](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/health/a62174647/optimal-daily-step-count/), reaching a certain daily step count is linked to a number of health benefits. Still, Wong says it's a moot point if you lack a consistent walking habit. 
Importantly, the sessions should be more than a casual stroll. “The key to these walks is that they need to be brisk. That's how you get the benefit,” emphasises [_W. Zach Smith, PT, DPT_](https://www.hidefpt.com/team/w.-zach-smith), founder of HIDEF Physical Therapy. “This will get the heart rate up into zone 2, which we now know based on the research is a great level of training for cardiovascular health.”
## How I Approached the 6-6-6 Walking Trend
I did the 6-6-6 walking challenge for a month, from November 22 and December 22. To monitor my progress, I used my phone’s Fitness app to track my steps. 
I wish I could say the walking itself was a delightful experience. Walking outside right as winter hit was, in hindsight, not fun. I would loop around the same block over and over instead of going to the nearby park. I wanted to be as close to my house as possible once the hour was up. 
## 6-Minute Warmup
My typical pre-workout stretches included dynamic moves that typically included: low lunge to hamstring stretch, downward dog to plank to cobra, arm circles, and glute bridge. I wouldn’t normally stretch before taking a walk, but doing it helped me wake up and get into the mindset of movement.
## 60 Minutes of Brisk Walking
The brisk walking pace was not a problem. It was all I could do to keep warm in the freezing weather. I don’t have an exact number of how fast I paced my walking throughout the hour, I moved based on the idea of [brisk walking](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/fitness/a63736125/walking-workouts-for-men/). I walked to the pace where I was breathing deeply but could still holding a conversation with someone. Though at 6 a.m., there was no one to talk to but myself.

My efforts paid off. In that one hour on the first day, iPhone’s Fitness app reported 6,240 steps at 2.88 miles. To put into context, the day before I had a grand total of 609 steps. Not even half a mile. (In my defense, I went to the gym).

Of course the motivation to brave the elements wore off after Week 2 and beyond. Instead I walked up and down a hallway of my house, much to the exasperated looks of my family.
Since I wasn’t on a treadmill nor would I torture myself to wake up even earlier to get to the gym, the brisk walking pace varied. Overall, my sessions ranged from about 4,200 to 6,300 steps during the 60 minute periods.

One thing stayed consistent after every walking experience – I immediately had more energy. When I walked at 6 a.m., the grogginess faded away. If I walked at 6 p.m., it got me out of my evening slump. 
Walking consistently also changed my state of mind. Starting the day already accomplishing a small goal kept me wanting to move more. I found myself finding any opportunity to walk and my daily step count would end up being between 8,000 to 12,000 steps. One day, I reached a total count of 17,190 steps. The only other time I've had a step count that high was when I traveled to Japan on vacation and walked everywhere.

## 6-Minute Cooldown
My favourite part – because it meant my session was over. Post-walk I would do six minutes of static stretches. This would usually involve: child’s pose, calf stretch, hip flexor lunge stretch, pigeon stretch, and lying spinal twist.
## The Verdict
I enjoyed my time doing the 6-6-6 trend, but it's not a perfect walking strategy. In the beginning, it was good to have a strict time commitment. But my busy schedule made the 6 a.m./6 p.m. rule became inconvenient. Many times I was tempted to push walking to noon or 8 p.m.

Another issue I came across: since I commuted to the office three times a week, I needed to walk at 6 a.m. on those days. And it went against my night owl [chronotype](https://www.menshealth.com/uk/nutrition/a63017009/does-meal-timing-matter/). Walking first thing in the morning messed with my sleep schedule. I use the sleep tracker app Rise Science, and it says I usually average seven hours of sleep. When I was doing the challenge however, I got less sleep on days I commuted. I was logging about four to six hours a night.
On the days I had shortened sleep, walking would give me energy to have a productive morning and afternoon. By evening I felt like a zombie. The rigidity of walking either at 6 a.m. or 6 p.m. messed with my gym schedule, feeling too exhausted to do my usual strength training. 
Still, walking is an excellent activity for general health. Wong says consistently walking at a brisk rate creates meaningful cardiovascular and metabolic benefits.
All things considered, I would do the 6-6-6 walking challenge again. It gave me structure and helped me build a consistent habit. The only thing I would change is being more flexible on _when_ I would actually walk, since movement, not rigid clock watching, is the real priority. Walking one hour a day is way less daunting than hitting a specific number of steps, and from my experience, much more manageable.
