## Abstract
E-cigarettes are a popular mode of delivery for nicotine, tobacco and cannabis. The prevalence of vaping among youth is increasing and this review aims to identify features of the neighbourhood environment, e.g., retailers, advertisements, and policies, that are associated with youth vaping. We included 48 studies. Of these, approximately 40% and 60% reported that presence of e-cigarette retailers, and advertisements, was associated with statistically higher odds of e-cigarette use in youth, respectively. Approximately 30% of studies reported that policies affecting e-cigarette availability were associated with statistically lower odds of vaping. Identifying these influential features of the neighbourhood environment will help formulate appropriate policies to reduce e-cigarette use among youth.

## 1. Introduction
E-cigarettes, also known as electronic cigarettes, electronic nicotine delivery systems (ENDS), and vapes, are an increasingly popular mode of delivery for nicotine, tobacco, cannabis, and/or flavouring and other chemicals ([World Health Organization, 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib76)). The devices were originally marketed as a less harmful alternative to traditional cigarettes and as an approach to support smoking cessation. However, e-cigarettes have become popular among young people, many who have never smoked tobacco ([Hammond et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib34)). The prevalence of vaping among youth has risen since 2016, particularly in countries where there are few regulations such as the USA and New Zealand ([Ball et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib4)). Vaping prevalence among young people in European countries ranged from 5.5% in Serbia to 41.4% in Monaco ([Cerrai et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib15)). The number of young people aged 16–19 years who reported using e-cigarettes at least once in the past week more than doubled in Canada and the United States between 2017 and 2019, from 28.8% in 2017 to 44.7% in 2019 and 36.3% in 2017 to 54.7% in 2019 respectively ([Hammond et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib34)). This is concerning because e-cigarette use may have harmful health effects, such as nicotine dependence ([Tobore, 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib66); [U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib69)). Research also suggests that adolescent e-cigarette users are more likely to subsequently initiate tobacco smoking ([O'Brien et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib53); [U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib69)). Therefore, understanding the predictors of e-cigarette use among young people is an important area of inquiry.
The neighbourhood environment, both physical and social, has been discussed and shown to be a significant predictor of tobacco smoking ([Cano and Wetter, 2014](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib14); [Ellaway and Macintyre, 2009](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib29); [Wiley and Seabrook, 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib72)). For example, the density and proximity of tobacco retailers in the built environment have been found to be associated with adolescent smoking and susceptibility to smoking ([Gwon et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib33); [Chan and Leatherdale, 2011](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib16); [Lipperman-Kreda et al., 2012](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib44)). Recent studies have identified similar features of neighbourhood environments that may also be associated with e-cigarette use, such as presence of retailers ([Bostean et al., 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib10); [Cole et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib19); [Giovenco et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib31)), advertisements ([Giovenco et al., 2015](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib32)), social characteristics ([Shih et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib63)), and policies ([Azagba et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib3); [Martin et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib48)). Increased advertising and marketing of e-cigarettes around schools can contribute to e-cigarette use among adolescents ([Giovenco et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib31)), and social factors such as neighbourhood disorganization and spending time with peers who engage in e-cigarette use have also been shown to contribute to e-cigarette use among adolescents ([Shih et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib63)). Policies, such as a ban on advertising, or those requiring licences in order to sell e-cigarettes, can also mitigate use of e-cigarettes among adolescents ([Azagba et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib3); [Martin et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib48)). Understanding these features and their impact on young people's e-cigarette use behaviours is important to inform new policies and processes that support health-promoting neighbourhood environments. The purpose of this systematic review is to identify those features of the neighbourhood environment that have been shown to be associated with current and ever e-cigarette use among young people with a focus on retailers, advertisements, social characteristics, and policies.
## 2. Methods
For the purposes of this review, neighbourhood environment is defined as a geographic area that young people may visit or interact with, such as around their home or school, and includes elements of the built and social environment, as well as the forces that shape those elements. This includes retailers, advertisements (e.g., billboards and newspapers), social characteristics (e.g., socioeconomic conditions), and policies, such as those restricting the sale or use of e-cigarettes. Ever use of e-cigarettes is defined as having ever used an e-cigarette, and current use is defined as having used an e-cigarette in the past 30 days or less.
### 2.1. Inclusion/Exclusion criteria
Studies were included if they were quantitative in design (including cross sectional, cohort, and case control studies), written in English, and published in academic journals after 2006—this date was selected as the cut-off because the first e-cigarette was successfully imported to the United States from China in 2006 ([U.S. Customs and Border Protection, 2006](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib68)). Studies were included if they incorporated a component of the neighbourhood environment and if they assessed current or ever use of e-cigarettes as the outcome, as defined above. The study population was young people, aged 10–24 years, as defined by the ([World Health Organization,](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib75)). Studies that were conducted on a different age group that overlapped with the age group of interest were included if the results were reported separately for those 10–24 years of age. Studies were not excluded based on geographic region.
### 2.2. Search strategy
This review followed the systematic review methods for a narrative synthesis as outlined by [Petticrew and Roberts (2006)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib59). A preliminary review was conducted to identify existing literature and systematic reviews, the research question was refined, and relevant search terms related to young people, neighbourhood environments, and e-cigarette use were determined. A final list of search terms was established by the authors ([Table 1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#tbl1)) and searches were conducted on January 13, 2021, and May 30, 2022, in eight databases: PubMed, SCOPUS, Web of Science, Sociological Abstracts, Nursing and Allied Health, PsychInfo, EMBASE, and CINAHL. The review protocol was registered with PROSPERO (ID: 243287; URL: [https://www.crd.york.ac.uk/prospero/display_record.php?RecordID=243287](https://www.crd.york.ac.uk/prospero/display_record.php?RecordID=243287)).

### 2.3. Study Selection process
Search results were uploaded to Covidence, and duplicates were removed. The screening process followed two stages: title and abstract screening, and full text screening ([Fig. 1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#fig1)). Documents were assessed according to the predetermined inclusion criteria. Initial title and abstract screening of studies was conducted by two reviewers [ZA, JG], and all studies that appeared to fit the inclusion criteria were maintained for full text screening, which was conducted independently by three reviewers [ZA, MI, JG]. Any disagreements between the three reviewers were resolved through discussion.

### 2.4. Data Extraction
Relevant data were extracted independently by three reviewers [ZA, MI, JG], compared, and compiled into an Excel spreadsheet. The information was used to summarize key characteristics and findings, which were organized by category of outcome (i.e., ever use or current use), neighbourhood characteristic (i.e., retailers, advertisements, social characteristics, and policies), and presence (i.e., exposure, accessibility, density, and availability). Studies that had findings pertaining to multiple outcomes, neighbourhood characteristics, or presence were listed multiple times.
Studies were organized into different categories of presence based on the terminology used by the authors. In terms of presence of advertisements, exposure was typically measured through self-reported exposure to an e-cigarette advertisement, where participants were asked whether, where, or how often they had seen notices or announcements that promoted e-cigarettes. In terms of presence of retailers, accessibility was defined as the ease at which a retailer can be reached from one's home or school. Although some studies may have defined accessibility differently, in general, the term “access” was used within the study to describe the potential exposure to the retailer. Density was defined as the number of retailers within a specified distance; the specified distance was not consistent between studies. Availability was defined as presence of a retailer within various distances of the home or school environment. The term “proximity” was used by two studies ([King et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib38); [Trapl et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib67)), defined as a measure of distance from one's home or school to a retailer, but the reviewers felt this overlapped closely with availability and categorized them as such. To assess presence of policies, availability was used and was defined as a jurisdiction having a policy implemented.
### 2.5. Quality Assessment
Study quality was assessed using quality assessment tools adapted by those developed by the [National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute (2021)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib78) and National Institutes of Health Quality Assessment Tools. To assess risk of bias and study quality, two reviewers (ZA, MI) independently used one of the quality assessment tools that was appropriate for the study design. Studies were rated on different elements in the tool, responding with either “yes”, “no”, “cannot determine”, “not reported”, or “not applicable”. Studies were then determined as either “good”, “fair”, or “poor” quality based on the responses to the elements in the tool. A “good” study was considered to have low risk of bias, while a “poor” study was considered to have a high risk of bias. Any discrepancy in ratings between the two reviewers were resolved through discussion until a consensus was achieved.
## 3. Results
The search strategy identified 10,264 records. Duplicates were removed, and those remaining (n = 6435) were screened for title and abstract eligibility. The full texts of 575 potentially eligible studies were obtained for further assessment, 48 of which met the inclusion criteria ([Fig. 1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#fig1)). A total of 18 studies were rated as “good”, 29 studies rated as “fair”, and 0 rated as “poor” ([Appendix A](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#appsec1)).
A total of 37 studies considered current use of e-cigarettes as an outcome ([Table 3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#tbl3)) and 22 examined ever use ([Table 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#tbl4)). Studies that considered both current and ever use (n = 12) were listed under both outcome types. The number of studies that explored retailers were 6 and 7 (current use and ever use, respectively); the number of studies that explored advertisements were 17 and 9 (current use and ever use, respectively); the number of studies that explored social characteristics were 5 and 1 (current use and ever use, respectively); and the number of studies that explored policies were 13 and 6 (current use and ever use, respectively) ([Table 2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#tbl2)).

### 3.1. Findings related to current use of E-cigarettes
#### 3.1.1. Retailers and current use
Six studies ([Bostean et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib9); [Cole et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib19); [Giovenco et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib31); [Mantey et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib47); [Perez et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib57); [Trapl et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib67)) explored the relationship between presence of retailers in the neighbourhood environment and current e-cigarette use ([Table 3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#tbl3)). Those studies accounted for 10 findings: four showed that presence of retailers were associated with statistically higher current e-cigarette use, and six showed no statistically significant associations. The findings are described below.
**Exposure**. One study ([Trapl et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib67)) explored exposure to retailers and current e-cigarette use which showed that increased visits to retailers was significantly associated with an increased odds of current e-cigarette use.
**Accessibility**. One study ([Mantey et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib47)) explored accessibility to retailers and current e-cigarette use which reported three findings: two findings showed that increased accessibility was significantly associated with increased relative risk of current e-cigarette use, and one finding showed no statistically significant association.
**Density**. Four studies explored density of retailers and current e-cigarette use: one ([Giovenco et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib31)) showed that increased density of retailers around schools was significantly associated with an increase in prevalence of current e-cigarette use; another ([Perez et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib57)) showed that students attending schools in tobacco retailer hot spots in some locations had a higher relative risk of current e-cigarette use, while other locations showed no associations; and two ([Cole et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib19); [Trapl et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib67)) showed no statistically significant associations.
**Availability**. Three studies ([Bostean et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib9); [Cole et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib19); [Trapl et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib67)) explored availability of retailers and current e-cigarette use, and all three showed no statistically significant associations between the presence, availability, or proximity of e-cigarette retailers and current use.
#### 3.1.2. Advertisements and current use
Seventeen studies ([Cho et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib18); [Cruz et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib21); [Dai and Hao, 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib23); [Giovenco et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib31); [Hammond et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib34); [Kreitzberg et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib39); [Kreitzberg et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib39); [Lee et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib43); [Marynak et al., 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib49); [Nicksic et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib51); [Papaleontiou et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib54); [Pasch et al., 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib55); [Peiper et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib56); [Perez et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib57); [Pesko and Robarts, 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib58); [Pu and Zhang, 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib60); [Simon et al., 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib64)) explored the relationship between advertisements in the neighbourhood environment and current e-cigarette use ([Table 3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#tbl3)). The types of neighbourhood environments included (from most common to least): retail environments, such as in or around kiosks, grocery stores, convenience stores, or drug stores; school environments; and outdoor environments, such as billboards. The 17 studies accounted for 27 findings: 17 showed that presence of advertisements were associated with statistically higher current e-cigarette use, and 10 showed no statistically significant associations. The findings are described below.
**Exposure**. All 17 studies explored self-reported exposure to advertisements and current e-cigarette use. Of the 26 total findings, 16 showed that presence of advertisements in retail, school, and outdoor environments were associated with statistically higher current e-cigarette use (i.e., current e-cigarette users were more likely to report exposure to advertisements than non-users); and 10 showed no statistically significant associations.
**Density**. One study ([Giovenco et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib31)), in addition to exploring self-reported exposure to advertisements, also explored density of advertisements around schools and current e-cigarette use. The finding showed that an increased density of e-cigarette advertisements was significantly associated with an increased probability of past-month e-cigarette use.
#### 3.1.3. Social characteristics and current use
Five studies ([Doggett et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib26); [Noland et al., 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib52); [O'Brien et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib53); [Pesko and Robarts, 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib58); [Wiggins et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib71)) explored the relationship between neighbourhood social characteristics and current e-cigarette use ([Table 3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#tbl3)). Those studies accounted for seven findings: four showed that various neighbourhood social characteristics were associated with statistically higher current e-cigarette use, one showed that various neighbourhood social characteristics were associated with statistically lower current e-cigarette use, and two showed no statistically significant associations. The categories of exposure, accessibility, density, and availability are not applicable to most of the findings described below.
**Density**. One study ([Doggett et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib26)) explored the effect of sports and recreation facilities on current e-cigarette use. The finding showed that an increased density of sports and recreation facilities in a school neighbourhood environment was significantly associated with a lower likelihood of current e-cigarette use.
**Other (urban vs. rural)**. Four studies ([Noland et al., 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib52); [O'Brien et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib53); [Pesko and Robarts, 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib58); [Wiggins et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib71)) explored the effect of urban versus rural dwelling or schooling on current e-cigarette use. Of the six findings, three showed that young people living in or attending schools in urban environments were more likely to be current e-cigarette users than young people living in or attending schools in rural environments, one ([Wiggins et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib71)) showed the reverse, and two ([Noland et al., 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib52); [Wiggins et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib71)) showed no statistically significant differences.
#### 3.1.4. Policies and current use
Thirteen studies ([Astor et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib2); [Azagba et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib3); [Dai et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib22); [Debchoudhury et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib25); [Du et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib28); [Hawkins et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib35); [Jun and Kim, 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib37); [Lee et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib42); [Macinko and Silver, 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib46); [Nguyen, 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib50); [Pesko and Robarts, 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib58); [Schiff et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib61); [Valentine et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib70)) explored the relationship between policies and current e-cigarette use ([Table 3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#tbl3)). Those studies accounted for 27 findings: three showed that policies were associated with statistically higher current e-cigarette use, eight showed that policies were associated with statistically lower current e-cigarette use, and 16 showed no statistically significant associations. The findings are described below.
**Availability**. All 13 studies explored the effect of policies on the availability of e-cigarettes and current e-cigarette use. Types of policies included: age restriction policies (such as T21 in the USA), taxation policies, retail licensing policies, use restrictions policies (such as smoke-free policies), and packaging and display policies. Of the 27 findings, three showed that policies were associated with statistically higher current e-cigarette use (i.e., current e-cigarette use increased after a policy was introduced that either limited or prevented access to e-cigarettes for young people), eight showed that policies were associated with statistically lower current e-cigarette use (i.e., current e-cigarette use decreased after a policy was introduced that either limited or prevented access to e-cigarettes for young people), and 16 showed no statistically significant associations.
### 3.2. Findings related to ever use of E-cigarettes
#### 3.2.1. Retailers and Ever Use
Seven studies ([Best et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib6); [Bigwanto et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib7); [Bostean et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib9); [Cole et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib19); [D'Angelo et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib24); [Giovenco et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib31); [King et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib38)) explored the relationship between presence of retailers in the neighbourhood environment and ever e-cigarette use ([Table 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#tbl4)). Those studies accounted for 10 findings: four showed that presence of retailers environment were associated with statistically higher ever e-cigarette use, and six showed no statistically significant associations. The findings are described below.
**Accessibility**. Two studies explored accessibility to retailers and ever e-cigarette use: one ([D'Angelo et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib24)) showed that an increase in visits to a convenience store was significantly associated with higher odds of e-cigarette ever use, and one ([Bigwanto et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib7)) showed no statistically significant association.
**Availability**. Four studies ([Best et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib6); [Bostean et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib9); [Cole et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib19); [King et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib38)) explored availability of retailers and ever e-cigarette use. Of the five findings, two ([Best et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib6); [Bostean et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib9)) showed that an increased availability of retailers was significantly associated with higher odds of e-cigarette ever use (e.g., young people who recalled seeing e-cigarettes in small shops and supermarkets were more likely to have tried e-cigarettes), and three ([Bostean et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib9); [Cole et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib19); [King et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib38)) showed no statistically significant association.
**Density**. Three studies explored density of retailers and ever e-cigarette use: one ([Giovenco et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib31)) showed that an increased density of e-cigarette retailers around schools was significantly associated with an increased probability of ever e-cigarette use, and two ([Cole et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib19); [King et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib38)) showed no statistically significant association.
#### 3.2.2. Advertisements and ever use
Eleven studies ([Beleva et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib5); [Camenga et al., 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib13); [Chen-Sankey et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib17); [Cho et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib18); [Dai and Hao, 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib23); [D'Angelo et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib24); [Giovenco et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib31); [Lee et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib43); [Loukas et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib45); [Nicksic et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib51); [Pasch et al., 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib55)) explored the relationship between advertisements in the neighbourhood environment and ever e-cigarette use ([Table 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#tbl4)). The types of neighbourhood environments included (from most common to least): retail environments, such as in or around kiosks, grocery stores, convenience stores, or drug stores; and outdoor environments, such as billboards. The 11 studies accounted for 22 findings: 12 showed that presence of advertisements were associated with statistically higher ever e-cigarette use, and 10 showed no statistically significant associations. The findings are described below.
**Exposure**. All 11 studies explored self-reported exposure to advertisements and ever e-cigarette use. Of the 21 findings, 12 showed that presence of advertisements in retail and outdoor environments were associated with statistically higher ever e-cigarette use (i.e., ever e-cigarette users were more likely to report exposure to advertisements than non-users), and nine showed no statistically significant associations.
**Density**. One study ([Giovenco et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib31)), in addition to exploring self-reported exposure to advertisements, also explored density of advertisements around schools and ever e-cigarette use. The finding, however, showed no statistically significant association.
#### 3.2.3. Social characteristics and ever use
One study ([Shih et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib63)) explored the relationship between neighbourhood social characteristics and ever e-cigarette use ([Table 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#tbl4)). That study accounted for four findings: two showed that perceived neighbourhood problems with alcohol or drugs and perceived neighbourhood disorganization was associated with statistically higher odds of past-year e-cigarette use, one showed that perceived increased neighbourhood cohesion was associated with statistically lower odds of past-year e-cigarette use, and one showed that objectively measured neighbourhood socioeconomic disadvantage was not significantly associated with past-year e-cigarette use. The categories of exposure, accessibility, density, and availability are not applicable to the above findings.
#### 3.2.4. Policies and ever use
Five studies ([Astor et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib2); [Borodovsky et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib8); [Dai et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib22); [Jun and Kim, 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib37); [Schiff et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib61)) explored the relationship between policies and ever e-cigarette use ([Table 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#tbl4)). Those studies accounted for 13 findings: three showed that policies were associated with statistically higher ever e-cigarette use, five showed that policies were associated with statistically lower ever e-cigarette use, and five showed no statistically significant associations. The findings are described below.
**Availability**. All five studies explored the effect of policies on the availability of e-cigarettes and ever e-cigarette use. Types of policies included: age restriction policies (such as T21 in the US), taxation policies, retail licensing policies, and packaging and display policies. Of the 13 findings, three showed that policies were associated with statistically higher ever e-cigarette use (i.e., ever e-cigarette use increased after a policy was introduced that either limited or prevented access to e-cigarettes for young people), five showed that policies were associated with statistically lower ever e-cigarette use (i.e., ever e-cigarette use decreased after a policy was introduced that either limited or prevented access to e-cigarettes for young people), and five showed no statistically significant associations.
**Density**. One study ([Borodovsky et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib8)), in addition to exploring the effect of policies on the availability of e-cigarettes, also explored the effect of policies on the density of e-cigarette retailers. This study could have been listed under the “Density” sub-section of “Retailers and Ever Use,” but it is listed here because the findings were discussed in relation to policies. The finding showed that an increased density of cannabis dispensaries was significantly associated with an increased probability of ever e-cigarette use.
## 4. Discussion
To our knowledge, this is the first systematic review to examine the relationship between elements of the neighbourhood environment and e-cigarette use among young people.
### 4.1. Retailers in the neighbourhood environment
Approximately 40% of the studies reported that presence of with e-cigarette retailers was associated with statistically higher odds of e-cigarette use in young people. It is difficult to directly compare these categories because the studies used different methods of measuring interactions with retailers, including exposure, accessibility, density, and availability. In addition, all studies relied on self-reported data, such as with the categories of exposure and accessibility, which may have introduced bias.
Our findings may be complicated by the types of e-cigarette retailers included in the studies. Some studies only examined specialty retailers ([Bostean et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib9)), when e-cigarettes are also commonly sold in convenience stores and gas stations. In addition, studies may be limited by the methods in which researchers obtained e-cigarette retail locations, such as online search engines ([Cole et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib19)), which may not capture all types of retailers.
Small local shops may be a significant source of e-cigarette retail exposure because they are frequently visited by young people and are an influential part of their environments ([Best et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib6)). Convenience stores, gas stations, and specialty e-cigarette shops are reported by youth to be the most common locations for e-cigarette advertisement exposure, especially in jurisdictions with less regulations ([Cho et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib18)). Given this, retail displays may normalize e-cigarettes to young people, making them seem socially acceptable and harmless, which has been observed with traditional tobacco cigarettes ([Brown and Moodie, 2009](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib12)). Additionally, exposure to e-cigarette retail makes their accessibility known to young people, a phenomenon that has also been documented relating to traditional smoking ([Doubeni et al., 2008](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib27)).
E-cigarettes were primarily sold via the internet when they first came on the market, but once the market expanded with lack of regulation, they became more available in traditional retail stores and sales grew most for conveniences stores ([Giovenco et al., 2015](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib32)). In many jurisdictions, there are age restrictions to purchase e-cigarettes and, if enforced, this may limit those under the legal age to purchase e-cigarettes at typical retailers. However, minors can easily circumvent age restrictions by purchasing e-cigarettes online where vendors have not implemented age-verification methods ([Williams et al., 2015](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib73)). Therefore, internet e-cigarette retailers may be a more convenient method of accessing e-cigarettes for young people than retailers in the neighbourhood environment. Social sources may also be a more convenient method of obtaining e-cigarettes, as acquisition through friends was reportedly the most common source ([Braak et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib11)).
Despite the mixed results of the relationship between presence of retailers and e-cigarette use, a previous review identified a correlation between density and proximity of tobacco retailers and adolescent smoking ([Gwon et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib33)). It is possible that with more research using objective measures, e-cigarette use may have the same association. For this reason, further regulation of e-cigarette retail in young people's environments is warranted.
### 4.2. Advertisements in the neighbourhood environment
Approximately 60% of the studies reported that presence of e-cigarette advertisements was associated with statistically higher odds of e-cigarette use in young people. However, it should be noted that just one study used an objective measure of advertising exposure ([Giovenco et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib31)), while all others relied on self-reported exposure data. [Giovenco et al. (2016)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib31) calculated the volume of e-cigarette advertisements within a half mile of schools (i.e., advertising density) to estimate potential exposure to advertisements and found an association with significantly greater e-cigarette use. While research has demonstrated a moderate association between more objective measures of exposure and self-reported exposure of retail advertising of traditional cigarettes ([Fong et al., 2006](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib30); [Henriksen et al., 2010](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib36)), self-reported data presents the potential for biases. [Loukas et al. (2019)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib45) explained that self-reported exposure may include informational content, rather than just advertisements. In addition, self-reported exposure mostly demonstrates conscious exposure, in which many experiences of exposure may not be recalled and are difficult to count ([Nicksic et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib51); [Shiffman et al., 2008](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib62)). Additionally, young people who use e-cigarettes are more likely to observe e-cigarette advertising while purchasing the devices and are more likely to be captivated by them ([Collins et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib20)). Given this, a correlation between self-reported advertising exposure and e-cigarette use would be likely.
Previous research identified that retail advertising exposure is the most common marketing channel, closely followed by internet or television/movie advertisements ([Camenga et al., 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib13); [Krishnan-Sarin et al., 2015](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib41); [Papaleontiou et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib54); [Singh et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib65)). There is scope for studies using objective measures of advertising. Neighbourhood audit techniques may be useful in gathering this information (e.g., [Martin et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib48)).
### 4.3. Neighbourhood social characteristics
Several neighbourhood social characteristics were examined for their relationship with e-cigarette use. Only one study examined ever-use and various neighbourhood social characteristics ([Shih et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib63)). Social characteristics perceived as negative (i.e., neighbourhood problems with alcohol/drugs, and neighbourhood disorganization) were associated with greater odds of e-cigarette use, while social characteristics perceived as positive (i.e., increased neighbourhood cohesion) were associated with less e-cigarette use. However, the single objective measure: ‘neighbourhood socioeconomic disadvantage’ was not associated with e-cigarette use. It might be that adolescents' perceptions of their neighbourhood's social characteristics are more influential in e-cigarette use than census-derived socioeconomic measures. However, with a lack of research on this element of young people's environments, it is difficult to draw conclusions. Nevertheless, multi-level interventions should be introduced to establish greater social cohesion in neighbourhoods ([Shih et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib63)). This is evident in the research by [Doggett et al. (2021)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib26) who found that increasing density of sports and recreation facilities around schools had a protective effect against e-cigarette use in young people.
Urbanicity demonstrated mixed results. Although rural communities have less dense built environments, and therefore fewer retail and outdoor advertisements, the tobacco industry has adopted sophisticated marketing methods (e.g., social media, television, and movies). In addition, due to economic pressures, rural communities may be less likely than urban communities to introduce smoke-free policies ([American Lung Association, 2012](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib1)). If this is also true for e-cigarette-related policies, rural residents may be subject to fewer restrictions for advertisements, purchasing, and using e-cigarettes. Thus, urbanicity may not be a critical environmental influence of e-cigarette use.
### 4.4. Policies in the neighbourhood environment
Approximately 30% of included studies reported that policies affecting the availability of e-cigarettes were associated with statistically lower odds of e-cigarette use in young people. A variety of policies were included, such as e-cigarette age restrictions, retail licenses, and taxation. The heterogeneity of policies made it difficult to draw overarching conclusions, but overall, policies were not significantly associated with current or ever e-cigarette use. Some policies that were significantly associated with e-cigarette use demonstrated a significant decrease in use after the policy was initiated, or significantly lower odds of use in jurisdictions that implemented policies, compared with those that had no policy in place, for example strict licensing laws for retail establishments and age-restriction policies ([Astor et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib2); [Azagba et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib3); [Jun and Kim, 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib37)).
The findings of one study ([Macinko and Silver, 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib46)) contradicted the authors’ hypothesis that e-cigarette use would decline following the introduction of a policy to increase the minimum legal purchase age for e-cigarette products in New York City. The authors postulate that either the policies were not robust enough or were rendered less effective due to poor retailer compliance. It is also likely that, due to the increasing popularity of e-cigarettes at the time of data collection, a policy limiting e-cigarette availability would not have a significant enough impact on the uptake of e-cigarettes by young people. Other possible explanations for the contradictory results include small sample sizes ([Jun and Kim, 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib37)) and short data collection periods. Regulatory policies limiting e-cigarette use are still changing and emerging, making it difficult to assess their long-term impact on young people.
Two policies—smoke-free policies for traditional tobacco products ([Hawkins et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib35)) and duration of cannabis legalization ([Borodovsky et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib8))—were associated with a significant increase in e-cigarette use. A possible explanation for this is that people are likely to seek an alternative and legal method of substance delivery, via e-cigarettes, where there are policies limiting the smoking of traditional cigarettes. Also, a longer duration of legalization of cannabis allows for more opportunity for marijuana e-cigarette use.
### 4.5. Limitations
There are two limitations of this review, both related to terminology: (1) there was no standard definition of “neighbourhood” used in the literature, meaning it may have been defined differently depending on the location or population; and (2) due to the rapidly changing discourse on e-cigarettes ([Cole et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib19)), and the many terms used to refer to e-cigarettes both academically and publicly, it is likely that some study participants may have misunderstood survey questions and either underreported or overreported on use. In addition, while not strictly limitations, the following should also be noted: studies in languages other than English, qualitative studies, and studies with a focus on e-cigarette use frequency were not included, which may have resulted in some significant findings being omitted; and nearly all studies used self-reported data to infer their results, opposed to biometric validation, which may have introduced bias. [Wong et al. (2012)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1353829224000406#bib74) found that self-reported data collected from young people on traditional cigarette smoking behaviours were accurate measures, but the same may not be true for e-cigarettes. Young people may be more reluctant to accurately report on their e-cigarette use for fear of recrimination or if the behaviour is seen as socially unacceptable; alternatively, young people may overreport their e-cigarette use if it is seen as socially acceptable or fashionable.
## 5. Conclusion
This review examines factors within the neighbourhood environment that could be predisposing young people to use e-cigarettes. More research using objective measures is needed. Longitudinal studies should be conducted to evaluate the long-term impact of policies, interventions, and features within the neighbourhood environment. Identifying potentially influential elements of the neighbourhood environment will aid in formulating policies to intervene, potentially reducing e-cigarette use among young people and thus promoting health.

  * ### [Exploration of Digital Interventions for Vaping Cessation: Scoping Review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1438887125014062)
2025, Journal of Medical Internet Research
Show abstract
Digital interventions have emerged as a promising approach to support vaping cessation, particularly among youth and young adults. Mobile apps, text messaging programs, telehealth-delivered contingency management, and web-based or social media interventions offer scalable and accessible alternatives to traditional cessation methods. However, there is considerable variation in how these interventions are designed, implemented, and evaluated, with inconsistencies in engagement strategies, theoretical frameworks, and long-term effectiveness.
This scoping review aimed to map the current landscape of digital interventions for vaping cessation and identify key strategies, effectiveness outcomes, and implementation challenges. The following questions were addressed: (1) What digital interventions have been developed or evaluated for vaping cessation? (2) What evidence exists regarding the effectiveness of these digital interventions in promoting vaping cessation and user engagement? (3) What key barriers and facilitators influence the adoption, adherence, and success of digital vaping cessation interventions? (4) What gaps remain in the literature, and what areas should future research prioritize to enhance the design and effectiveness of digital vaping cessation tools?
This scoping review followed the Joanna Briggs Institute methodology and adhered to the PRISMA-ScR (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews) guidelines. A systematic search was conducted in CINAHL, MEDLINE, and PsycINFO using search terms related to vaping cessation and digital health interventions. Studies examining mobile apps, text messaging programs, social media or web-based interventions, and telehealth coaching explicitly designed for vaping cessation were included. A narrative synthesis was conducted to identify common themes, barriers, and facilitators.
Sixteen studies were identified, including SMS text messaging programs, mobile apps, telehealth-delivered contingency management, and web-based or social media interventions. Many interventions reported moderate to high abstinence rates. Programs incorporating personalized messaging, behavioral tracking, and social and interactive features demonstrated greater retention and cessation success. However, minimal application of evidence-based behavior change frameworks, inconsistent reporting of engagement metrics, reliance on self-reported abstinence, and scalability limitations were noted.
Digital interventions show promise for vaping cessation, particularly among youth and young adults, but current evidence highlights both opportunities and limitations. Effective interventions leverage personalization and social support to enhance engagement and quit outcomes. However, challenges such as high dropout rates, accessibility barriers, and limited use of rigorous evaluation methods persist.
