## What’s New in Apple Music iOS 26.4
Apple’s five new Apple Music additions mix long-overdue fixes, a bold UI swing, and an Apple Intelligence playlist tool. And they’re all shipped together like a coordinated shot at Spotify.

  * [Concert discovery lands in artist profiles](https://www.headphonesty.com/2026/02/apple-music-dropping-update-vs-spotify/#Concert_discovery_lands_in_artist_profiles)
  * [Background music moves to the Home Screen](https://www.headphonesty.com/2026/02/apple-music-dropping-update-vs-spotify/#Background_music_moves_to_the_Home_Screen)
  * [A louder, more polarizing UI overhaul](https://www.headphonesty.com/2026/02/apple-music-dropping-update-vs-spotify/#A_louder_more_polarizing_UI_overhaul)
  * [Type a prompt, get 25 songs](https://www.headphonesty.com/2026/02/apple-music-dropping-update-vs-spotify/#Type_a_prompt_get_25_songs)


### Multi-playlist song additions
A new selection button lets you pick multiple songs and add them to multiple playlists at once. This replaces the one-track-at-a-time routine that playlist builders have lived with for years.
It won’t make anyone switch services by itself, sure, but the absence has been a persistent papercut for power users, and this removes it.
### Concert discovery lands in artist profiles
Artist pages now surface upcoming concerts with venue details and ticket links, which is a feature Spotify has had for years.
This is less about novelty than retention. Besides, if a music app can help connect listening to real-world events, it becomes stickier, even if the feature reads like simple parity.
### Background music moves to the Home Screen
Apple’s Ambient Music feature moves from its hiding spot in Control Center to a Home Screen widget. This widget comes in small or medium sizes and lets users jump into four mood categories (Chill, Productivity, Sleep, and Wellbeing) without opening the app.
This matters because it reframes what “using” Apple Music looks like. Spotify built its empire on active listening, on playlists curated for specific tastes and moments.
Apple’s bet is that a huge share of listening time is effectively _background infrastructure_ , where the sound follows you through AirPods, HomePods, and the Home Screen with minimal decision-making.
That’s a bet on where streaming minutes actually live.
### A louder, more polarizing UI overhaul
Apple Music’s new interface just became more colorful and seamless. (From: BetaProfiles)
Apple Music’s visuals get a major refresh with a full-screen album view that pulls colors from artwork to tint the background. And, the Liquid Glass design language applied to menus and pop-ups.
It’s the most dramatic interface change in years and it’s also where the update becomes contentious. Early reaction has focused on readability and simplicity, with some users arguing the new look prioritizes style over clarity.
Liquid Glass can be disabled in settings, but the default experience prioritizes visual spectacle over readability. 
### Type a prompt, get 25 songs
[Playlist Playground](https://routenote.com/blog/apple-music-tests-ai-playlist-playground-feature/) is an AI playlist generator powered by Apple Intelligence. With this, you can just type a prompt and get a **25-song** playlist. From there, you can swap tracks, refine with follow-up requests, create custom cover artwork, and share finished playlists publicly on their profiles.
The custom artwork and public sharing give it a social layer that Spotify’s equivalent lacks.
However, it’s also deliberately gated. Apple Intelligence limits access to iPhone 15 Pro or later, iPads with M1 or later, and M-series Macs. This makes the Playlist Playground as much an advertisement for newer hardware as a streaming tool.
## How Apple’s AI Playlists Compare to Other Platforms
AI playlist generation isn’t unique to Apple. In fact, Apple is the fourth major streaming platform that released it as Amazon, Spotify, and YouTube Music have all rolled out some version of the same idea over the past six months.
Amazon got there first with Weekly Vibe in September 2025, and it remains the only AI playlist tool that doesn’t require a paid subscription. By January 2026, [Spotify had expanded Prompted Playlist](https://newsroom.spotify.com/2026-01-22/prompted-playlists-expansion/) from its New Zealand beta to the US and Canada. YouTube Music followed in February 2026, right before Apple Music. 
Out of all these versions, Spotify’s still feels like the most mature. It supports conversational refinements, adds short explanations for each track, and can refresh playlists daily or weekly. Plus, it also includes an “Ideas” feature that suggests prompts tied to cultural moments.
Step-by-step process on how to use AI playlist. (From: Spotify)
Meanwhile, Apple’s Playlist Playground leans more into presentation and sharing. It offers custom cover art, public profiles, and Apple Intelligence integration. But it still outputs a fixed 25-song playlist, which makes it less flexible than Spotify’s more iterative approach.
Spotify knows it. Its [Global Head of Editorial, Sulinna Ong](https://www.linkedin.com/in/sulinna), framed Prompted Playlist as “a more intuitive entry point” built on moods, moments, and user language rather than rigid automation. The pitch is collaboration, not a feature checkbox.
In practice, however, the competitive edge may be smaller than the feature lists suggest. Beta testers were blunt about the dynamic:
“Cool feature that they’ve copied from Spotify. I like it,” [one user wrote](https://forums.macrumors.com/threads/apple-music-playlist-playground-in-ios-26-4-creates-playlists-from-text-prompts.2477800/).
Both halves of that verdict matter. Users recognize Apple arrived last to AI playlists, and they don’t care. Others pointed to practical scenarios, like generating an office playlist on demand for a group with mixed tastes, where the “who did it first” question fades fast.
Copied or not, the new features mostly get Apple to the table. The sharper edge is pricing, and Spotify just handed it over.
## Spotify’s Price Hikes Give Apple an Opening
Spotify [raised prices for the third time in three years](https://9to5mac.com/2026/02/13/apple-music-pokes-fun-at-latest-spotify-price-increase/) in February. Premium climbed from $11.99 to $12.99, Family from $19.99 to $21.99.
On the other hand, Apple Music hasn’t moved from [$10.99 individual and $16.99 family](https://9to5mac.com/2026/02/13/apple-music-pokes-fun-at-latest-spotify-price-increase/) since October 2022. And it made sure nobody missed the gap.
> “BTW, we’re still the same price,” [Apple Music posted on X](https://techgenyz.com/apple-music-boldly-reacts-to-spotify-price-hike/).
That’s $2 per month cheaper on individual plans, $5 on family, the widest pricing gap since both services launched. Over a year, the difference adds up to $24 or $60 depending on the tier.
Apple reinforced the message with a [three-month free trial running through February 24](https://offers.applemusic.apple/super-bowl-offer). Spotify, meanwhile, seems comfortable trading backlash for revenue.
A J[.P. Morgan estimate](https://cord-cutters.gadgethacks.com/news/spotify-price-hike-2026-third-increase-in-3-years/) suggests each $1/month increase can generate roughly $500 million in annual revenue. 
Apple backed up the jab with a [three-month free trial](https://9to5mac.com/2026/02/13/apple-music-pokes-fun-at-latest-spotify-price-increase/) through February 24. Spotify keeps hiking regardless. Morgan estimates](https://cord-cutters.gadgethacks.com/news/spotify-price-hike-2026-third-increase-in-3-years/) each $1 monthly bump generates roughly $500 million in annual revenue, a tradeoff the company can clearly live with.
Pricing is Apple’s sharpest weapon, but it’s not the only front. Apple is also widening the fight into the format Spotify has been pushing hardest: video.
[Apple Podcasts is launching native video podcasting](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/02/16/apple-takes-on-youtube-and-spotify-with-new-video-podcasting-push.html) this spring, as a direct catch-up to Spotify’s video push, but with an Apple-specific twist.
Apple Music has [carried music videos for years](https://www.headphonesty.com/2021/04/review-apple-music/), so video isn’t a new competency inside the ecosystem. What’s new is bringing that video behavior into podcasts, where Spotify has been trying to own the conversation.
Then there’s Apple’s revived social layer, [Music Connec](https://musicconnect.apple.com/)t, which reads like a reputational jab as much as a feature.
Apple is positioning distribution as free for creators, leaning into the contrast with Spotify’s long-running criticism around artist treatment and creator economics.
So, even if the mechanics differ by format and business model, the implication is intentional. It wants to look like the platform that doesn’t make creators pay for access, and to make Spotify defend its image while it’s already defending its prices.
The ambition is obvious, but the math is less encouraging. Apple Music has [94 million subscribers](https://www.demandsage.com/apple-music-statistics/) to Spotify’s [290 million](https://variety.com/2026/digital/news/spotify-q4-2025-earnings-record-monthly-users-year-of-raising-ambition-1236657863/), a gap that no single update will erase.
But Apple isn’t swinging for a knockout. It’s trying to make the fight interesting.
